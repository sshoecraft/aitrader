description: Size from the worst drawdown you can survive; sizing scales return AND drawdown together and never improves risk-adjusted odds.

---

Sizing is not alpha. Turning size up moves the bad days as hard as the good ones — Sharpe is unchanged. So choose size from the drawdown you can *survive*, not the return you want. A mathematically optimal high-return path can be humanly unsurvivable: an 88-90% drawdown, or a 39% one landing in year four of a five-year horizon, is reason to reject regardless of CAGR. The path you cannot sit through is not a path you own. Weigh the worst plausible stretch — and *when* it could land — against your real horizon. Before committing to any approach, imagine its worst single day (a correlated multi-position shock, an adverse gap) and check your sizing survives it. If one bad day wipes you out, sizing is too aggressive no matter how good the average case looks.

Leverage amplifies whatever is really there. It converts a Sharpe edge into return ONLY when the edge is genuinely robust; on a marginal or noisy signal it just multiplies drawdowns and decay (2x/3x trend overlays repeatedly tied or lost to the index at worse drawdown — "levered beta, not alpha"; the *same* insider trades that merely underperformed as stock were capital-destroying as options: 40% win rate, 23% wipeouts). Size a leveraged product *smaller* for the same conviction, because a move against you hits proportionally harder. Earn the right to leverage by first confirming the edge is robust — never lever to make a thin edge look bigger.

Adding to a winner (pyramiding) is a deliberate concentration decision, not a default. It deploys capital at a worse average cost after the easy move is captured, ties up capital that could fund an independent idea, and raises variance without raising expected return; on a high-quality selection process it tended to *hurt* total dollars. It only pays when a protective discipline has already culled non-winners so the added size chases confirmed strength. Critically: every add raises your blended cost, so your real break-even and downside are measured from the *average*, not the first entry. An exit that looks "in profit" from where you started can sit below your average and hand back the whole gain (SLB: 10 adds, ~$4,700 unrealized peak given back). An averaged-up basis corrupts "progress vs entry" reasoning — recognize when your own reference point is muddied before acting. Never assume a stop makes scaling-in safe.

Let equity dial *sizing and concurrency*, not what counts as a good trade. A 1-share position is a sign your sizing logic broke, not a real trade — size to thesis and survivable risk, not to whatever cash is left over.
