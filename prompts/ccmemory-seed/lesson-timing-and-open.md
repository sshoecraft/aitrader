Time-of-day and holding-horizon are first-class inputs to a decision, not afterthoughts. Where in the day a position lives, and how long you hold it, can decide whether an edge exists at all. Apply all of this as judgment against the live tape — never as a clock to set or a number to gate on.

**The open is a distinct regime, but its first prints are noise.** Overnight gaps and the first range carry real information about fresh demand and repricing. But the very first prints are dominated by whipsaw and gap-and-fade fakeouts — in one study the genuine since-open leaders only separated from the noise after the opening shake-out. If you need conviction a mover is real rather than an opening artifact, let the tape settle before acting. The open is context worth attention, never an automatic breakout trigger.

**Don't chase a move that already happened.** Buying a name that already gapped up or already made its big morning move tends to perform *worse* — in the cleanest data the gapped-up subset had the lowest win rate, persistently negative across every cut. Paying up after a jump often means buying others' exit liquidity. Ask whether the move is extended away from any support you'd lean on (chasing far from support gives a worse entry and less room on a snap-back), and whether there's still room and a reason — or whether you're simply late.

**Intraday momentum is real but thin, and its edge decays through the day.** Persistent strength in a liquid name is genuine evidence of continuation, but it's a weak statistical tilt, not a per-trade win — the same strength means you're buying late. The continuation edge is strongest early in the session and bleeds away as the day wears on; being late to a momentum trade is materially worse than being late to a slow-moving thesis. Weigh a late entry on a momentum thesis far more skeptically than an early one, and demand a concrete reason *this* move keeps going before chasing.

**Match thesis horizon to holding horizon.** Many momentum/ranked setups carry a genuine open-to-close intraday edge that is fully given back over multi-day holds — the intraday winners and the multi-day-hold winners were almost entirely disjoint sets. Conversely US equity returns have historically accrued overwhelmingly overnight, with the intraday session roughly flat. A position doesn't earn uniformly around the clock: ask where in time the return you want shows up, and let that decide when to be exposed. An edge real on one timescale can be ruinous on another. Validate against your own fills — old anomalies arbitrage tighter, and they're asset-specific (overnight drift doesn't transfer to crypto).

**Repeated same-day losses are a signal to disengage.** A book that kept queuing fresh entries through a continuous descent compounded into a blown day (the "Opening Bell Fiasco": seven same-day round trips, all stopped, while it kept buying the decline). When you've been stopped out repeatedly today, treat that as evidence the environment is hostile *right now* and step back. Accumulating same-day losses is information, not noise to trade through.

**Don't open what you can't reliably flatten.** If a thesis needs you out by a specific time, confirm the real session close with a holiday/half-day-aware calendar — naive time math falsely reports "open" on no-session days. Confirm the venue is actually open and able to fill before sending; a market order into a closed market gets parked and a retry loop can stack hundreds of duplicates that all fill at once on reopen (one incident stacked over a thousand overnight). Treat a pending order as still-live, not a failure to re-fire. Start deadline exits early with margin — broker calls hang, and a stalled query during the close has stranded positions overnight. In thin/after-hours liquidity expect plain market orders to reject and a last-print limit to just sit; getting out usually means conceding a little to cross the spread, which matters more than the last fraction.

**Calibrate to the instrument and session.** A move means something only relative to the symbol's own intraday volatility — small can be a real leg in a low-vol index yet noise in a single stock. 24/5 forex and 24/7 crypto have no overnight gap, so don't import equity gap intuition there; FX reverts over weeks and rarely needs minute-by-minute babysitting. Intraday round-trips also consume a limited day-trade budget that can block the very exits your method depends on — patience between trades is usually cheaper than the impulse to stay constantly in the market.
