Futures are not equities with a different ticker. The hazards below can end an account, not just dent a P&L.

**Expiry/roll/delivery is the account-ending mechanic.** Every contract expires, and liquidity drains from the front month into its last sessions — volume thins, spreads widen, so rolling late means trading a hostile book. Physically-settled contracts (crude, nat gas, metals, grains, softs) can stick a long-only account with mandatory physical delivery if held to settlement — catastrophic and irreversible. Cash-settled index futures (ES/NQ/YM) avoid delivery but still suffer the roll liquidity drop. Always know time-to-expiry on anything you hold; roll or exit well before the front month's final sessions. Never let a position drift into delivery territory because you weren't watching the calendar. Futures trade on margin: a move against you can force liquidation regardless of thesis. The forward curve carries cost — contango charges you to hold, backwardation pays roll yield.

**Match the lens to the contract — futures are not one thing.** Index futures (ES/NQ/YM) behave like their index and tend to mean-revert short-run; chasing breakouts here has disappointed, buyable dips fit better. Commodities are driven by supply/demand, seasonality, the curve — softs and grains more often run real sustained moves (riding fits; fundamentals lead, technicals confirm). Rate futures (ZB/ZN) are dominated by Fed policy and don't travel far — big directional theses rarely pay. In the winners study softs/grains had far higher winner:loser ratios than equity indexes; rates produced essentially no large directional winners. Treat this as personality, not a ranking to mechanically apply.

**Regime overrides the instrument prior.** The "index futures mean-revert, fade strength" instinct is actively wrong during macro/supply-shock trends. Feb–Apr 2026 Iran-war crude: a reversion lens stayed short through a +42% runup ($71→$94), then issued BUYs at $91 and $94.69 right before −8% to −11% single-day drops. Before leaning on "overbought," ask: ordinary range/chart structure, or a geopolitical/macro shock? Shocks run far past where reversion says they should — fading them is how you get run over.

**Open interest reads conviction.** A move on expanding OI in its direction is fresh money (real). A rally on shrinking OI is mostly short-covering (weak); a decline on shrinking OI is longs bailing (weak) — exhaustion tells, not trend confirmation.

**Crowding / COT (weekly context, not a trigger).** When speculators pile in at multi-year extremes, the easy money that side is likely done — lean contrarian; commercial hedgers at extremes carry opposite information. A rally persisting while committed players quietly reduce is distribution, not a green light (silver's fund positioning fell during its rally — a smart-money exit tell).

**Data/evidence discipline.** Continuous back-adjusted series (Yahoo `=F`) are stitched at each roll, so indicators near roll dates can reflect the adjustment, not real price — reason on the actual front-month contract and distrust signals around roll dates. Our futures evidence base is thin: a clean OOS test on a small sample (13 train / 22 test) showed negative alpha at high conviction. That proves we can't confirm an edge, not that futures don't work. "I can't confirm it works" is not "it works." Don't pool short-history with long-established names or one class with another into one verdict — segment before generalizing. On a class where your basis is genuinely thin, standing aside is the honest move. Calibrate your "did it work" yardstick to how far the specific market actually travels — a bar built for fast softs unfairly fails slow rates.
