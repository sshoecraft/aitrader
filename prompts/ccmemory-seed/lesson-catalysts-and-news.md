description: A catalyst is WHY price moves; the tape never tells you why. Ask what scheduled/breaking event could kill each thesis before price reflects it.

  type: reference
---

Price is not risk. The tape shows you *that* something is moving and how fast — it can never tell you *why*. Catalyst reasoning is the dimension price structurally hides, so it is where your judgment earns its keep.

**The catalyst veto is the one check price can't give.** Selecting a name on momentum or chart shape is blind to fraud, accounting clouds, halts, an imminent earnings print, or a broken business. Clearing the chart does not clear the company. The faster/more mechanical your reason for looking, the harder you must cover the catalyst risk the mechanics can't see. When you vet an extreme mover, don't re-litigate the technicals that made it a mover — they're maxed by construction and carry no accept/reject signal. Aim scrutiny at the orthogonal question: catalyst, news, earnings, halt, fraud.

**A held position is exposed to catalysts a stop can't see.** A stop only reacts *after* the tape moves; it cannot see a known event coming. The prior system lost five figures when energy names tanked on a tariff deadline that price-only monitoring never anticipated. So keep asking what scheduled or breaking event could destroy each thesis, and treat a confirmed thesis-killing catalyst as its own reason to act. (This is judgment about *looking* — not an auto-sell timer.) Know which risk a safeguard covers: a weekend flatten does nothing for a mid-week shock headline.

**Catalyst on a clean base, not a headline alone.** Gaps fade; a headline by itself is unreliable. The durable winners paired a real catalyst *with* a constructive base. For multi-day holds, weigh whether a real fundamental reason exists for the move to continue.

**Earnings surprises drift, slowly.** Markets under-react — price keeps drifting in the surprise's direction afterward (PEAD, Bernard & Thomas 1989; weaker recently, strongest in small/lightly-covered names). This was the one effect that survived honest out-of-sample testing when pure price strategies collapsed into beta. The negative-surprise mirror underperformed symmetrically — that control is *why* it's believable. The first-day gap is often un-tradeable (gone, noisy, widest spread); the capturable part is the continuation. Treat a fresh surprise as an ongoing thesis to monitor — never "buy any surprise, hold N days."

**Catalyst data is perishable and load-bearing.** A misdated event injects look-ahead; a stale feed acts on yesterday's reality. Verify the event-date source. If inputs are degraded, stand down — doing nothing is legitimate. For delayed signals (e.g. Congress disclosure lags, median ~1 month, mean far longer), ask if the move is already priced; entering on disclosure date failed to beat the index.

**Don't let an untested news filter tax opportunity.** A "require a news catalyst" gate, never tested, silently discarded ~half of candidates with no measured benefit. If you can't point to evidence a requirement earns its rejections, leave it off. (Contrast real frictions like a liquidity floor — worth keeping.)

**Long options across an event:** holding premium through a scheduled catalyst invites IV crush — IV deflates right after, so a correct directional call can still lose if you overpaid for pre-event vol.
