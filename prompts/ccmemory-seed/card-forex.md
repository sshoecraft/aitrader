Per-asset depth for forex. The cross-asset judgment is in the constitution; this is what FX adds — and the FX direction-of-edge is the OPPOSITE of equities, so it overrides the equity instinct.

Forex behaves opposite to equities. The most heavily evidenced finding in the archive: FX winners are oversold, below-trend, beaten-down pairs; FX losers are overbought breakout-chasers. Over 12 years of daily bars across 36 pairs (214 winners vs 378 losers), winners sat well below their range while losers were stretched above (parabolic extension AUC 0.986); winners were oversold, losers overbought (RSI AUC 0.950); winners far from 20-day highs (AUC 0.938). Rising momentum was a majority-loser signal. So when an FX pair looks like it's "breaking out strongly to new highs," treat that as a flag to argue AGAINST, not a reason to enter — your equity momentum reflex will systematically buy the loser in FX. FX reverts over weeks, not days, so don't apply an equity-momentum clock or assume a thesis failed because it hasn't paid in a few days. Caveat: majors carry structural trend drivers (rate differentials/carry) and flip between trending and ranging regimes; read the regime, but the default disposition is reversion.

A pair is a bet on TWO currencies (long EURUSD = EUR strong and/or USD weak) — reason about both legs, favor strong-against-weak. The account-ending failure is hidden correlation: positions sharing a quote currency, funding currency, or macro driver are ONE correlated bet — in a risk-off shock they all move adversely and stop out together. In the archive, 7 of 10 pairs were JPY-quoted; when JPY surged on risk-off/BOJ tightening the concurrent JPY longs hit stops within days, producing -56% to -85% clustered drawdowns. The book that treated the cluster as one bet held max drawdown to ~2%. Carry books are the acute case: positive-carry pairs are all bets the low-rate funding currency stays weak, and they unwind together in a panic.

Carry is a real but small tailwind (low single-digit % annualized), NOT protection — a positive-carry pair stops out fine on a price drop (AUD/JPY did). In the best configs ~84-87% of gross P&L was passive carry while clever entry timing added zero or negative price P&L; if nearly all profit traces to carry you have a carry trade in costume, not a timing edge. Carry is regime-dependent and perishable: it only pays under wide policy-rate dispersion (a ZIRP period lost vs a high-dispersion period that won — Sharpe swing over 1.0), and rests on current CB rates that move at meetings — re-check around CB events before leaning on it.

Match stops to the instrument's own noise. FX daily ranges are small but choppy, so a stop sized for stock noise sits inside normal FX wiggle and gets harvested by chop — a primary driver of a -84% drawdown. The fix isn't a different number: a thesis-break exit or scheduled re-evaluation protected capital better, and in the lowest direction-volatility cases holding to horizon with no protective stop outperformed. Before relying on a stop, ask whether its distance exceeds the pair's ordinary churn or just guarantees noise-stopping.

Gotchas: "volume" is tick volume (OTC, no central tape) — a rough activity proxy, not confirmation; lean on price structure. No intraday gaps (24/5 continuous) so equity gap reasoning doesn't apply; only the Fri-close/Sun-reopen weekend gap, which is a real unmanageable exposure window (Sunday opens gapping 50-150 pips). IBKR merges all currency cash, so FX positions can't be reconstructed from balances (cross pairs break it) — journal FX state explicitly from your fills. A bare 6-char USD ticker may be forex or crypto — use broker asset class, not string shape. Spreads are far worse in thin Asian hours and best at London-NY overlap — let session liquidity inform when you pull the trigger.

Epistemic discipline specific to FX: plausible macro narratives aren't edge — "FX trades with the dollar/DXY" tested three ways over 11 years as indistinguishable from random (AUC ~0.50). An ADX-bullish signal was negatively correlated with P&L because it kept selecting counter-regime entries. A 0.95+ AUC classifier separating curated extreme winners from losers produced negative P&L bar-by-bar live. You compete with hedgers, central banks, prop desks, and HFTs — a long-only no-leverage account has a narrow honest opportunity set; pick the few edges that survive your constraints.
