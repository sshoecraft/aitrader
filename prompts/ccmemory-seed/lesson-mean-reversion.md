"Overbought/oversold" is not an edge by itself — context decides whether reversion is your friend or the thing that runs you over.

**An oversold bounce or pullback is only trustworthy when the larger structure is still advancing.** Buying "oversold" inside a confirmed downtrend is catching a falling knife — that's how you get repeatedly stopped, not an edge. When the bigger picture is down, demand a much stronger, specific reason before fading it.

**Never fade a macro/supply/geopolitical-shock trend.** "Fade it, it's stretched" is fine for ordinary range-bound structure and actively wrong during a catalyst-driven run. A reversion read once shorted crude's entire war-driven advance, then flagged "buy" right before sharp drops near the top — extension features misread shock momentum as something to mean-revert. Decide range-like vs shock-driven first; in a shock, "looks stretched" is a weak reason to fade.

**Which way edge points flips by asset class — never carry one asset's instinct into another.** Equities/index-stocks reward strength (breakouts, trends, coiled-near-highs); chasing the parabolic extension was the loser's signature. Forex is the opposite — over 12yr/36 pairs the winners were oversold, below-trend, far-from-highs; rising-momentum FX pairs were far more often losers (one of the strongest separations measured). Index futures and rates short-run mean-revert (failed breakouts dominate); softs/grains run directionally. Crypto run-ups have ~a two-week shelf life — harvest, don't marry.

**Pullback vs reversal is texture, not a level.** Buyable: gradual overlapping bars, declining volume, shallow, holding support, higher lows. Reversal: sharp gaps, expanding down-volume, slicing structure, lower lows, the whole group breaking. A "test" that makes a lower low on heavy volume has become a reversal.

**Best entries come from a reset, not a chase** — consolidation-then-resumption, failed breaks (springs/upthrusts), coiled-but-not-blown-out. Among the day's strongest movers, the ones still ripping into your entry fade more.

**Treat anomalies and backtests as hypotheses to disprove.** The dead-cat bounce showed ~zero edge once costed (t-stat indistinguishable from noise). Beaten-down names carry bid-ask-bounce illusions and intraday pops that fade by the close — measure against a price you could actually transact. A reversion entry with only a tight stop and a clock structurally bleeds (noise stops winners, survivors drift back to entry); every position needs a reasoned way to realize a gain, and an exit that ratchets faster than the edge can develop clips winners while losers run.
