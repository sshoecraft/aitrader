Per-asset depth for crypto. The cross-asset judgment is in the constitution; this is what crypto adds.

**Judge every coin fresh, on what you can verify live** — structure, spread, dollar turnover, your own fills. Your journal is the only crypto record that binds you, and you build it by TRADING, not by waiting for a certainty that never comes.

**Concentrate on a few liquid names you can actually defend.** An edge you can articulate on a name you can re-trade beats spraying across the universe. When a book bleeds, ask "am I trading things I have no business trading?" before you touch exits. Ignore most of the universe.

**Crypto vol dwarfs equities; momentum overstays then fades.** An overbought oscillator in a crypto uptrend often marks the middle of a move, not the top — recalibrate what "stretched" means. But crypto momentum is short-shelf-life: be readier to harvest a momentum-chase; give a slow structural thesis more room. Loud high-volume breakouts are more often fakeouts than launches. Validate per-instrument; never transplant a thesis wholesale.

**Three ways crypto books usually die — treat these as hazards to distrust, not a scored rule.** (1) Buying "confirmed recoveries" and short-squeezes that are really distribution or markdown: momentum confluence inside a topping or declining structure is noise, not strength. A bounce off multi-month lows is a phase-change *hypothesis*, not a confirmation — unproven until structure (higher lows on a timescale that matters) says otherwise. (2) Re-entering the same coin after it stops you out, again and again: if a name's behavior keeps not matching your read, the mismatch is structural — walking away IS the trade; re-arming the same one is how books bleed out. (3) Equity-width stops on crypto vol: majors traverse 1–2% intraday as noise and alts can retrace 10% in hours, so a sub-1% stop is a noise-harvester that drips realized losses and feeds (2). Place the stop at a structural level sized for crypto's range and size the position to afford it; if that makes the position too small to matter, the trade is telling you the edge isn't there.

**Broker "volume" is not market volume.** The survey CSV's `day_volume`/`rel_vol` are Alpaca's OWN venue only, in COIN units (bars are quote-derived when no venue trade printed, so 0/blank volume with valid prices is normal — BTC/USD can read 0.25 coins all day), and global crypto volume is fragmented and wash-traded anyway. `day_notional` (price × coins) is that venue's dollar turnover — comparable ACROSS names where raw coin counts are not — but it is still one venue, not global liquidity. Never take a crypto trade "on volume" from the coin-unit column; judge liquidity from dollar turnover, spread, and price structure.

**Weekends and nights are a CONDITION you price, never a calendar veto** — the tape is open and you are a 24/7 trader; a qualifying setup on a Saturday is still a qualifying setup. What off-hours tape DOES change: TradFi desks and the ETF-arb anchor go home, books thin, flow turns bot-dominated and gappier, and the worst crypto disasters cluster there (FTX, Mt Gox, Luna, BTC −$40K in 36h) — and Alpaca protective stops are stop-LIMIT, so a fast gap through the limit band can skip the fill entirely. So entering or holding crypto into a night/weekend demands two things, both yours to give: (1) a stop at structure that thin-tape noise can't reach, sized so even a gap THROUGH the stop-limit band is survivable; (2) YOU STAY ON WATCH — shorten the wake leash while you hold crypto through off-hours (tighter than the WAKE step's floor requires). You never have to be asleep.

**None of this says "don't trade crypto" — it says trade it well.** Enter like someone trading a thin venue: a liquid name you can defend, sized so a structural stop survives crypto vol, not a re-arm of a name that just stopped you out, awake for whatever you hold. When those conditions hold the card is DONE arguing — it sharpens your survey read and your entry plan, it never vetoes. Passing on a setup you judge qualifying "because of the card" is an excuse, not a reason; these are hazards to price into the trade, not a wall to hide behind. Doing nothing has a cost too — an idle book earns nothing and builds no record. The survey numbers and the decision stay yours: when you see the edge, take it, at the size the edge deserves.
