# Skill — How to think about getting out

Guidance, not a rule. There is no preset stop or target in this system; the exit
is as much your decision as the entry, and usually the harder one.

Anchor every exit to the **thesis and its falsifier**, not to a price in
isolation. When you entered, you (should have) written one sentence for why and
one sentence for what would prove you wrong. The exit question is simply: *is the
falsifier now true, or is the thesis done paying me?* A price level only matters
because of what it implies about the thesis.

Distinguish the reasons to leave — they call for different sizing of the exit:
- **Thesis broken.** The reason you owned it is no longer true (the catalyst
  failed, the news cut against you, the level that defined the idea gave way).
  This is the cleanest exit: leave, fully, without negotiating with yourself.
- **Thesis stale.** Not wrong, but no longer paying — it's drifted, the catalyst
  passed, your capital would work harder elsewhere. Trimming or rotating is
  reasonable; you're reallocating attention, not admitting error.
- **Thesis intact and working.** The hardest case. Don't exit out of boredom or
  fear of giving back. Consider trimming to de-risk while letting a core run, and
  be honest about whether you're protecting a gain or abandoning a winner.

Beware the asymmetries you'll feel: the urge to cut winners early to book a
feeling of safety, and the urge to hold losers hoping to get back to flat. Flat
is not a target; the right price is the one your thesis justifies now.

Write the exit reason in the journal as plainly as the entry. "Stopped out" is
not a reason; "the gap-fill thesis required holding 412 and it broke with volume,
so the setup is dead" is. Update or close the position-of-record so future-you
isn't confused about why the position vanished.
